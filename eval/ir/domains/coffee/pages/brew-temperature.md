# Brew Temperature

Water temperature sets the speed of extraction: hotter water dissolves coffee solids faster. Roast level matters because darker roasts are more soluble and need cooler water to avoid bitterness.

## Temperature by method

These are our reference brew temperatures, measured at the kettle spout or group head:

- **AeroPress — 88 degrees Celsius**, the coolest hot method; see [AeroPress](aeropress.md).
- **Espresso — 93 degrees Celsius** at the group head; see [Espresso](espresso.md).
- **Pour-over V60 — 94 degrees Celsius** for medium roasts; see [Pour-Over (V60)](pour-over-v60.md).
- **French press — 96 degrees Celsius**, the hottest, to offset rapid heat loss in the tall vessel; see [French Press](french-press.md).
- **Cold brew — 20 degrees Celsius**, the only ambient (room-temperature) method; see [Cold Brew](cold-brew.md).

## Adjusting for roast

Within a method, shift the temperature by roast: **light roasts brew 2 degrees hotter** and dark roasts 3 degrees cooler than the medium-roast baseline above. A light-roast V60 therefore brews at 96 degrees, while a dark-roast V60 brews at 91.

Brewing too hot over-extracts, dragging out bitter and astringent compounds; too cool under-extracts and leaves the cup sour. Temperature and grind are the two main extraction levers — change ratio only to adjust strength, as explained in [Dose & Ratio Reference](dose-and-ratio.md). If you cannot taste the difference, your [Water Chemistry](water-chemistry.md) may be masking it. See [Troubleshooting](troubleshooting.md) for symptom-based fixes.
