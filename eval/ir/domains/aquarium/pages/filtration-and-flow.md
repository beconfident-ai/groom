# Filtration and Water Flow

Filtration in a planted tank serves two masters: biological stability and even distribution of [CO2](co2-injection.md) and nutrients. Size the filter for a turnover rate of 8 times the tank volume per hour — a 40 litre tank wants a canister rated for at least 320 litres per hour. Anything below 5x turnover leaves dead spots where detritus settles and [algae](algae-control.md) takes hold.

A canister filter is strongly preferred over a hang-on-back unit because it disturbs the surface less, conserving dissolved CO2. Layer the media from coarse to fine in the direction of flow: coarse sponge first to trap debris, then biomedia (sintered glass or ceramic rings) for the bacterial colony that powers the [nitrogen cycle](cycling-the-tank.md), and fine floss last to polish the water. Skip carbon, which strips the trace [fertilizers](fertilization.md) you dose.

Aim the outflow along the back glass so flow circles the tank and returns along the front, reaching every leaf. A lily pipe or spray bar tuned just below the surface gives gentle, even movement without a churning surface.

Clean the mechanical sponge monthly in a bucket of old tank water, never under the tap — chlorine kills the beneficial bacteria. Do not clean biomedia and sponge in the same week; staggering preserves the colony. A filter that gurgles or slows has a clogged intake; this is the most common cause of a sudden flow drop, so check it during the weekly [maintenance routine](maintenance-schedule.md).
