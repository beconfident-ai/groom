# Troubleshooting

Most brewing faults trace back to extraction. Diagnose by taste first, then change one variable at a time. The order of adjustment is always **grind, then temperature, then ratio** — grind is the strongest lever.

## Sour, sharp, or salty (under-extraction)

A sour cup is **under-extracted**, below the 18 percent floor from [Extraction & TDS Measurement](extraction-tds.md). Fix it by **grinding finer**, raising temperature toward the [Brew Temperature](brew-temperature.md) target, or extending brew time. On a V60, a sour cup usually means drawdown finished before 2:15.

## Bitter, dry, or hollow (over-extraction)

A bitter, astringent cup is **over-extracted**, above 22 percent. **Grind coarser**, lower the water temperature, or shorten contact time. On espresso, bitterness with a slow drip means the shot ran past 32 seconds — grind coarser.

## Weak or watery

Weak coffee that is not sour is a **strength** problem, not extraction. Lower the ratio number (use more coffee) per [Dose & Ratio Reference](dose-and-ratio.md). Hollow, flat cups can also come from low-mineral water; check the 150 ppm TDS target on [Water Chemistry](water-chemistry.md).

## Channeling and uneven extraction

**Channeling** is water carving a fast path through the bed, over-extracting some grounds and under-extracting the rest at once — the result tastes both sour and bitter. Cure it with even distribution: a WDT tool and level tamp for espresso, a flat bed and gentle bloom for V60. Inconsistent particle size from a dull or blade grinder is a common root cause; review [Grinders & Equipment](equipment.md) and the [Grind Size Guide](grind-size.md).
