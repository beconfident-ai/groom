# Water Chemistry

Brewed coffee is over 98 percent water, so water mineral content directly shapes flavor. Minerals act as solvents that bind to coffee compounds; too few and the cup is hollow, too many and it tastes chalky and dull.

## Targets

Our reference brewing water follows these targets:

- **Total dissolved solids (TDS): 150 ppm**, measured with a TDS meter.
- **Total hardness: 68 ppm as calcium carbonate** (the magnesium and calcium that drive extraction).
- **Alkalinity (buffer): 40 ppm as calcium carbonate**, which controls how much acidity the water neutralizes.
- **pH: 7.0**, neutral.

Never brew with distilled or zero-TDS water — it extracts unevenly and tastes empty. Equally, hard tap water above 200 ppm mutes brightness and scales equipment.

## DIY mineral recipe

To build water from distilled, we add a concentrate and dose **one drop per liter** of our two-part mineral solution, hitting the 68 ppm hardness target above. Magnesium-forward water accentuates fruit and acidity; calcium-forward water emphasizes body.

High alkalinity above 50 ppm flattens the bright, sour notes you want from a light-roast V60; low alkalinity lets acidity sing but can make under-extracted coffee taste harsh. Because water chemistry shifts perceived acidity, always rule it out before re-grinding — see [Troubleshooting](troubleshooting.md). Water TDS is distinct from beverage TDS; the [Extraction & TDS Measurement](extraction-tds.md) page keeps the two separate. Match your [Brew Temperature](brew-temperature.md) to the roast once water is dialed in.
